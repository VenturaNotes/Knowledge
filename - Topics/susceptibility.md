## Source[^2]
- (1) (magnetic susceptibility) (symbol: $\chi_{\mathrm{m}}$) A dimensionless quantity given by$$\chi_{\mathrm{m}} = \mu_{\mathrm{r}} - 1$$where $\mu_{\mathrm{r}}$ is the relative permeability of a material. Magnetic susceptibility describes the response of a material to a magnetic field, being the ratio of magnetization to magnetic field strength:$$\chi_{\mathrm{m}} = \frac{M}{H}$$$\chi_{\mathrm{m}}$ is a tensor when $M$ is not parallel to $H$, otherwise it is a simple number. For crystalline material $\chi_{\mathrm{m}}$ may depend on the direction of the field with respect to the crystal axes because of anisotropic effects. It has a wide range of values: diamagnetic materials have a negative value; paramagnetic materials (see PARAMAGNETISM) have a small positive value; ferromagnetic materials can have a very large variable value (up to about one), which is dependent on the magnetic field strength (see MAGNETIC HYSTERESIS). 
- (2) (electric susceptibility) (symbol: $\chi_{\mathrm{e}}$) A dimensionless quantity given by$$\chi_{\mathrm{e}} = \varepsilon_{\mathrm{r}} - 1$$where $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{r}}$ is the relative permittivity. Electric susceptibility measures the ease of polarization of a dielectric and is given by the ratio$$\frac{\chi_{\mathrm{e}} = P}{\varepsilon_{0} E}$$where $P$ is the dielectric polarization, $E$ the electric field strength, and $\varepsilon_{0}$ the permittivity of free space.
