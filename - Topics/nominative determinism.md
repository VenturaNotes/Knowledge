## Source [^1]
- $n$. The notion that people's names can influence their career choices and other aspects of their lives. Carl Gustav Jung (1875-1961) gave several examples in an article on synchronicity published in 1952. A study by the Canadian psychologist Ernest L(awrence) Abel (born 1943) published in the journal Names in 2010 compared doctors and lawyers whose first or last names began with three-letter combinations representative of their professions, such as Doc or Law, and found a significant relationship between names and professions; also, initial letters of physicians' last names were significantly related to their sub-specialty, men called Raymond being significantly more likely to be radiologists than dermatologists, for example. See also aPtronym. \[Coined in an article in the British magazine New Scientist in 1994, citing a book on polar exploration by Daniel Snowman and an article on incontinence in the British Journal of Urology in 1977 by the urologists J. W. Splatt and D. Weedon]
