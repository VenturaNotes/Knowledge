- Chinese remainder theorem Let$$m_{1}, m_{2}, \ldots, m_{r}$$be positive integers that are relatively prime to one another, and let their product be $m$ :$$m=m_{1} m_{2} \ldots m_{r}$$Let $n, u_{1}, u_{2}, \ldots, u_{r}$ be integers; then there is exactly one integer, $u$, that satisfies$$n \leq u<(m+n)$$and$$u \equiv u_{j}\left(\text { modulo } m_{j}\right) \text { for } 1 \leq j \leq r$$
## Source[^2]
- For coprime natural numbers $m$ and $n, \mathbb{Z}_{mn}$ is isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_m \times \mathbb{Z}_n$ as a ring. More concretely, there is unique $x$ in the range $0 \le x < mn$ that solves the simultaneous congruences $x \equiv a \pmod m$ and $x \equiv b \pmod n$. The isomorphism from $\mathbb{Z}_{mn}$ to $\mathbb{Z}_m \times \mathbb{Z}_n$ sends $x \pmod{mn}$ to $(x \pmod m, x \pmod n)$. If $u, v$ are integers such that $um + vn = 1$ (see BÉZOUT'S LEMMA), then the inverse sends $(a \pmod m, b \pmod n)$ to $vna + umb \pmod{mn}$.
- More generally, if $I$ and $J$ are coprime ideals of a ring $R$ then$$\frac{R}{I \cap J} \cong \frac{R}{I} \times \frac{R}{J}.$$
- This can be proved by applying the first isomorphism theorem to the homomorphism $r \mapsto (r+I, r+J)$.
