## Source [^1]
- A transistor circuit in which the current in one side of the circuit is forced to be identical to the current in the other side of the circuit: a 'mirroring' action. The simplest current mirror comprises just two transistors, the diagram showing an example using bipolar junction transistors.
- Because the bases and emitters of the two transistors are connected together, the base-emitter voltages must be identical. If the transistors are the same, then the collector currents must be the same. The input transistor has the base and collector connected together $\textendash$ a diode-connected transistor $\textendash$ allowing its base-emitter voltage to be determined explicitly. The output of the current mirror is taken as the collector load of the second transistor, providing a high-impedance current source output that is relatively insensitive to loads.
- More complex current mirror circuits have been devised that provide even greater insensitivity to loading, an example being the Wilson current mirror.
