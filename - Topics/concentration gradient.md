## Source [^1]
- (diffusion gradient) The difference in concentration between a region of a solution or gas that has a high density of particles and a region that has a relatively lower density of particles. By random motion, particles will move from the area of high concentration towards the area of low concentration, by the process of diffusion, until the particles are evenly distributed in the solution or gas.

## Source[^3]
- The presence, in a volume region, of a differing density of particles across that volume. In semiconductor devices there are two kinds of concentration gradient. The first is the fixed variation in the concentration of n-type and p-type impurities as an intended consequence of their manufacture. The second arises as the impurities lead to the generation of a corresponding concentration gradient associated with the mobile carrier density. This leads to diffusion currents that work to flatten this gradient, and it is the interaction of these with voltage-driven drift currents that determines how the device operates. See N-TYPE SEMICONDUCTIVITY; P-TYPE SEMICONDUCTIVITY.
