## Source [^1]
- A sequence of random variables $x_{1}, \ldots$, $x_{n}, \ldots$ converges in probability to a random variable $x$ if for every positive number $\varepsilon$ the probability of the (Euclidean) distance between $x_{n}$ and $x$ exceeding $\varepsilon$ converges to zero as $n$ tends to infinity, so$$x=p \lim x_{n} \Leftrightarrow x_{n} \xrightarrow{p} x \Leftrightarrow \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} P\left[\left|x_{n}-x\right| \geq \varepsilon\right]=0 \Leftrightarrow \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} P\left[\left|x_{n}-x\right| \leq \varepsilon\right]=1$$for every $\varepsilon>0$. This means that, if we consider a sequence of probabilities, $P_{n}=P\left[\left|x_{n}-x\right| \geq \varepsilon\right]$, then starting from some $n_{0}$ each probability in this sequence is arbitrarily small. In particular, $x$ can be a constant. Convergence in probability implies convergence in distribution (the converse does not hold, in general).
