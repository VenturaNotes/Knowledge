## Source [^1]
- Oscillations arising in a circuit under the influence of internal forces, such as a capacitor discharging through a resistance and inductance, or of a constant external force, such as a direct voltage. Both these conditions are analogous to a mechanical vibrating system being displaced from the neutral point.
- The oscillations decay gradually, depending on the amount of damping in the circuit, with a frequency, $f$, termed the natural frequency; this is approximately equal to $(LC)^{-2}$, where $L$ is the inductance and $C$ is the capacitance, when the resistance $R$ in the circuit is small (see diagram). The amplitude of the current is given by$$i = A \mathrm{e}^{-\left(\frac{R}{2L}\right)t} \sin \omega t$$where the angular frequency $\omega$ is equal to $2\pi f$, and $A$ is a constant determined by the initial conditions. The maxima of successive oscillations lie on the curve$$i = A \mathrm{e}^{-\left(\frac{R}{2L}\right)t}$$and the amplitudes $(i_{\mathrm{m}}, i_{\mathrm{m+1}})$ of successive maxima of the same sign decrease by a constant ratio. It can be shown that$$\log_{\mathrm{e}} \left(\frac{i_{\mathrm{m}}}{i_{\mathrm{m+1}}}\right) = \frac{\pi}{Q}$$where $Q$, the Q factor of the circuit, is given by$$Q ^ {2} = \frac {L}{R ^ {2} C}$$Compare FORCED OSCILLATIONS.
