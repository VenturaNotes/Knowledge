## Source [^1]
- The irreducible *characters of a finite *group $G$ tend to be listed in a character table. This concisely represents a lot of information about $G$ and its *normal subgroups. As an example the character table of $S_4$ is:

|          | $e$ | $(123)$ | $(12)(34)$ | $(12)$ | $(1234)$ |
| :------- | :-: | :-----: | :--------: | :----: | :------: |
| $\chi_1$ |  1  |    1    |     1      |   1    |    1     |
| $\chi_2$ |  1  |    1    |     1      |   -1   |    -1    |
| $\chi_3$ |  3  |   -0    |     -1     |   1    |    -1    |
| $\chi_4$ |  3  |    0    |     -1     |   -1   |    1     |
| $\chi_5$ |  2  |   -1    |     2      |   0    |    0     |
- #errata In the original table, these intersections were incorrect / mislabelled
	- $\chi_3$ and $(123)$ should be 0, not -0
	- $\chi_3$ and $(12)$ should be 1, not -1
- Characters are constant on *conjugacy classes, so one representative per class is shown. There are as many *irreducible representations (rows) as there are conjugacy classes (columns). The degrees of the characters appear in the first column, and the *order of $G$ is the sum of their squares; here $24 = 1 + 1 + 9 + 9 + 4$. The first listed character is the trivial character, associated with the representation $\varphi(g) = I_1$ for all $g$.
