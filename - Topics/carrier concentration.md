## Source [^1]
- (carrier density) The number of charge carriers in a semiconductor per unit volume, in practice usually quoted as numbers per cubic centimeter. In an intrinsic (i-type) semiconductor the number of holes, $p$, and of electrons, $n$, is equal to the intrinsic density, $n_{\mathrm{i}}$:$$n = p = n _ {\mathrm {i}}$$where$$n _ {\mathrm {i}} ^ {2} = N _ {\mathrm {c}} N _ {\mathrm {v}} \exp \left(- \frac {E _ {\mathrm {g}}}{k T}\right)$$where $N_{\mathrm{c}}$ and $N_{\mathrm{v}}$ are the effective densities of energy states in the conduction and valence bands respectively, $E_{\mathrm{g}}$ is the difference in energy between the conduction and valence bands, $k$ is the Boltzmann constant, and $T$ the thermodynamic temperature.
- In an extrinsic semiconductor the electrical neutrality of the sample is preserved and in a sample that contains impurities,$$N _ {\mathrm {A}} ^ {-} + n = N _ {\mathrm {D}} ^ {+} + p$$where $N_{\mathrm{A}}^{-}$ and $N_{\mathrm{D}}^{+}$ are the numbers of ionized acceptor and donor impurities, respectively. At relatively high temperatures most of the impurity atoms are ionized and it is possible to state that$$n + N _ {\mathrm {A}} = p + N _ {\mathrm {D}}$$where $N_{\mathrm{A}}$ and $N_{\mathrm{D}}$ are the total numbers of acceptors and donors. The product $np = n_{\mathrm{i}}^{2}$ is independent of added impurities.
- In an n-type semiconductor the number of electrons, $n_{\mathrm{n}}$ , at thermal equilibrium is given by$$n _ {\mathrm {n}} n = \left| N _ {\mathrm {D}} - N _ {\mathrm {A}} \right|$$provided $$N_D > N_A >> n_i$$
