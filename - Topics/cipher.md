## Source[^2]
- An algorithm used for encryption of information or, in its inverse form, for decryption. It is one of the earliest forms of encryption. Used by Julius Caesar during the Gallic wars, Caesar cipher employed a simple alphabetic shift in which the transmitted letter was the desired letter shifted a number of places in the alphabet. For example, to transmit 'many' using a two-letter shift results in the transmission of 'ocpa' – each letter is shifted two places along the alphabet with wrap around at z. To decrypt the code all that is required is the number of shifts.
## Source[^3]
- A primitive means of encrypting a message—the $26$ letters of the alphabet are permuted and every occurrence of a particular letter in a text is substituted for its replacement to make the ciphertext. A very basic example is Caesar ciphers, where each letter is shifted by some fixed amount to find its replacement, for example $ABCD \dots$ being replaced by $RSTU \dots$. Even though there are $26! > 4 \times 10^{26}$ ciphers, frequency analysis can quickly reduce the possibilities, and so ciphers are easy to decrypt.
