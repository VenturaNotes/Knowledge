## Source [^1]
- (quality factor) (symbol: $Q$) A factor that is associated with a resonant circuit and describes both the ability of the circuit to produce a large output at the resonant frequency and the selectivity of the circuit. The Q factor is defined as$$\frac{2\pi \times \text{energy stored}}{\text{energy dissipated per cycle}}$$or alternatively as$$\frac{\text{energy stored}}{\text{energy dissipated per radian}}$$In the case of a simple series resonant circuit at the resonant frequency,$$\omega_0 L = \frac{1}{\omega_0 C}$$and the Q factor is given by$$Q = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R} \quad \text{or} \quad Q = \frac{1}{\omega_0 C R}$$where $\omega_0$ is $2\pi$ times the resonant frequency $f_0$ and $L$, $C$, and $R$ are respectively the inductance, capacitance, and resistance of the circuit.
- The selectivity of a resonant circuit can be written as$$Q = \frac{f_0}{\mathrm{BW}}$$where BW is the difference in frequency between the two 3 dB points.
- A single reactive component may be capable of resonance without any other components, i.e. if the self-capacitance of an inductance coil or the self-inductance of a capacitor is sufficiently large. The Q factor of a single component is defined as the ratio of the reactance to the effective series resistance of the component, hence for an inductance$$Q = \frac {\omega_ {0} L}{R}$$and for a capacitor$$Q = \frac {1}{\omega_ {0} C R}$$
