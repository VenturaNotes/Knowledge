## Synthesis
- CamelCase is where a capital letter is used in the middle of a word to mark the start of a new part of the name
	- #question Isn't camel casing when the first word is lowercase and the remaining letters are uppercase?
		- For example, you have `myFirstVariable`
- #question What is orthographic branding (if that is even a term)?
- Companies like **MathWorks**, **YouTube**, and **FedEx** use "medial capitals" (capitals in the middle) to create a visual identity that is distinct and easy to read at a glance.
	- Useful for [[trademark|trademarking]] purposes or domain names (`YouTube.com`)
		- #question What is a trademark?
		- #question But I thought domain names are not case sensitive? 
- Writing an academic paper 50 years ago may have required a company name in standard Title Case such as `Mathworks` but modern grammar and style guides like AP or Chicago follows the "Official Name Rule"
	- #question What is Title Case? 
	- #question What are the main differences between AP and Chicago?
	- #question How did AP and Chicago come to be?
	- #question Is the "Official Name Rule" a real thing?
- The "Official Name Rule" is
	- You should write a proper noun exactly how the entity itself styles it.
- So `YouTube` is the proper way to write it
- Once a name is legalized or trademarked, that specific arrangement of letters and cases becomes the correct grammar for that specific noun.
